Lone Wolf?

David Copeland is often described as a paradigmatic case of a lone wolf terrorist, as he apparently did not tell anyone about his plans to carry out the attack and worked completely on his own. However, according to “End of the Lone Wolf: The Typology that Should Not Have Been” by Schuurman et al., the typology of the “lone wolf” is very misleading. Besides a handful of high-profile cases, most so-called lone actor terrorists maintain many extremist social ties, have poor operational security, and show signs and hints of their intentions to carry out an attack long before they do. The authors note that David Copeland is an example of a terrorist who worked alone simply because he was unable to create stable social connections with others prior to the attack. They state that he “may have tried to form a neo-Nazi terror cell before deciding to strike out alone” (Schuurman et al., 2018). While we were unable to find another source stating this, we know that Copeland was a member of the National Socialist Movement (NSM) at the time of the bombings.

We may never know exactly why he decided to plan and carry out the bombings by himself, but we do know that he did not plan this in a vacuum. Through his involvement with the British National Party (BNP) and later the NSM, Copeland became used to and familiar with right-wing extremist thought. Schuurman et al. write that an individual has to overcome their own moral barriers to commit acts of violence against others, and that extremist ideologies and groups can help this process move more quickly. Neo-nazi groups like the NSM are known for their endorsement of violence as a tactic, and no doubt being a part of this group influenced Copeland in his convictions. According to Copeland, his main motivation for the attacks was to start a race war, which he took directly from The Turner Diaries, a famous work of white supremacist dystopian fiction. The rhetoric of a race war to preserve the white war was also likely inspired by his time in the BNP and NSM, as this is a common white supremacist and right-wing extremist talking point. Copeland also read online guides about how to make bombs, further demonstrating the importance of external sources in radicalization and inspiration for attacks.

While Copeland may have acted alone, he would not have acted at all if the idea of terrorism as a means of achieving a political goal had not been introduced and normalized through his involvement in right-wing extremist and Nazi parties and spaces. Therefore, he is not a “lone wolf.”


Precipitants + Preconditions

In the case of David Copeland, his preconditions included coming from a working-class background, being uncomfortable with homosexuality and feeling insecure about his own sexuality, having divorced parents, having social trouble at school, and being socially isolated.

His precipitants were his dissatisfaction with the electoral politics of the BNP and the normalization of violence he witnessed while in the NSM. Most specifically, the upcoming elections motivated Copeland, as he stated in his confession, “My aim was political. It was to cause a racial war in this country. There'd be a backlash from the ethnic minorities, then all the white people will go out and vote BNP” (BBC, 2021). His direct goal was to influence the elections by starting a race war.


Strain Theory

Strain theory focuses on the impact of stressors, or “strains,” on an individual, which may cause them to commit crimes. This theory can be applied to David Copeland because of his divorced parents, difficulties with maintaining a job, social difficulties as a child, and insecurity about his sexuality. All of these factors may have led him to choose to join extremist groups and then commit violent attacks. The attacks can be seen as ways to “escape his circumstances” (Campbell, 2024). Because of the political motivation behind the attacks, this theory alone does not suffice to explain these bombings.
